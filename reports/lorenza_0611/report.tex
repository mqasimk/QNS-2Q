\section{How much dephasing? The anchored model}\label{sec:model}

The synthetic spectra used previously were replaced by a model anchored to
measured silicon and GaAs dephasing data: the six-spectrum structure and
charge-noise correlations of the Tarucha-group Si/SiGe pair [Yoneda \textit{et
al.}, Nat.\ Phys.\ \textbf{19}, 1793 (2023)], purified-$^{28}$Si coherence as the
amplitude anchor, high-frequency exponents from natural-Si spectroscopy
[Rojas-Arias \textit{et al.}], nuclear-Larmor-difference lines from GaAs
[Malinowski \textit{et al.}, Nat.\ Nano.\ \textbf{12}, 16 (2017)], and exchange
noise at the operating point [Dial \textit{et al.}, PRL \textbf{110}, 146804
(2013)]. The ``featured'' regime adds the line triplet to the monotonic
(``bland'') background.

\section{Characterization: the reconstruction lands on truth}\label{sec:char}

\textbf{No-SPAM pipeline (4k shots, $t$-grain 1600).} The full blind
reconstruction --- harmonic comb inversion, line/tail/head-aware unfolding, DC
points from noise-aware slope fits and the double-echo $S_{12,12}(0)$ experiment
--- is statistically consistent with truth at honestly quoted uncertainties
(Table~\ref{tab:recon}). At 64k shots (the SPAM-arm runs) the same pipeline
reaches 1--6\% median deviations with the same pull structure.

\section{Ask 2: do I need all six spectra?}\label{sec:ask2}

The answer splits cleanly into \emph{error budgeting} and \emph{control design}:

\textbf{(a) For the error budget --- yes.} Undecoupled, the two-qubit channels
($S_{12,12}$ and the cross-spectra) carry only $1.2\%$ of the gate error at
$\TG=320\tau$. Under the best decoupling, CDD(7,7), they carry
\textbf{20.4\%}: decoupling suppresses the single-qubit channels so effectively
that the two-qubit channels are promoted to a fifth of the residual error
budget. Without two-qubit QNS you cannot predict or budget a decoupled gate's
error.

\textbf{(b) For the optimizer --- largely no, and that is a feature.} We re-ran
the blind optimization with progressively reduced spectral knowledge (the ideal
benchmark always keeps full truth); Fig.~\ref{fig:asks}(left) and
Table~\ref{tab:subsets}. The blind NT winner is essentially unchanged down to
single-qubit-only knowledge at both gate times: dropping the cross-spectra changes nothing at all, and even
dropping $S_{12,12}$ costs only $6\%$ at $\TG=80\tau$ (and nothing at
$320\tau$) --- the single-qubit spectra dominate the design, and the CZ phase
constraint pins the $y_{12}$ filter's DC weight regardless of what the optimizer
knows about $S_{12,12}$. In particular, the \textbf{SPAM-robust protocol's
four-spectrum set costs nothing at the gate level} --- which couples directly
into ask 3.

\section{Ask 3: why characterize SPAM?}\label{sec:ask3}

Two regimes, now both measured:

\textbf{Spectroscopy: SPAM bias is large and certain.} At 64k shots the raw
arm's reconstruction is wrong at 14--34$\sigma$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:spam}) --- any
spectroscopic claim made from unmitigated data is falsified at high confidence.
SPAM mitigation restores truth ($\equiv$ reference within bars), and the
double-echo experiment turns $S_{12,12}(0)$ from a bounded into a determined
quantity ($1.12\times10^{-4}$ vs.\ truth $1.11\times10^{-4}$).

\textbf{Gates: the design is forgiving; the prediction is not.} Optimizing on
the raw arm's biased spectra produces a gate \emph{identical in true performance}
to the reference-arm gate at $\TG=320\tau$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:asks}, right): both
arms find the same NT(148,150) sequence at true $1-\Fpro = 4.27\times10^{-2}$.
The smooth SPAM bias does not move the optimal filter placement. What SPAM
characterization buys at the gate level is the \emph{credibility of the
predicted} numbers: the reference arm predicts its gate to $0.7\%$, the raw arm
to $1.9\%$ at this gate time --- and the uncertainty bands of \S\ref{sec:gates}
are only meaningful with unbiased spectra and honest error bars.

Synthesis with ask 2: since the robust protocol's lost cross-spectra do not
affect the gate design, \textbf{SPAM-robust QNS $+$ NT optimization is a
self-sufficient pipeline} when one accepts budget-level (not channel-resolved)
error accounting; SPAM-mitigated QNS recovers the full six-spectrum budget.

\section{The three answers, in talk form}

\textbf{Ask 1 --- how much dephasing?} $T_2^{*} = 20\,\mu$s ($800\tau$) anchored
to purified $^{28}$Si; Gaussian FID $T_2 \approx 19\,\mu$s; gates studied span
$0.05$--$3.3\,T_2$. Spectra: $1/f$-type charge noise (exponents 0.4--0.7, Si and
GaAs agree), GaAs nuclear-difference line triplet at 1.7/1.7/3.4 MHz, exchange
noise at $10\%$ amplitude coupling.

\textbf{Ask 2 --- do I need all six spectra?} For the error budget, yes:
decoupling promotes the two-qubit channels from $1\%$ to $20\%$ of the gate
error. For the optimizer, no: the blind NT design is unchanged down to
single-qubit knowledge ($\leq 1\%$ effect) --- so the robust protocol's
four-spectrum set is design-complete, and two-qubit QNS is what makes the
\emph{prediction} and the budget honest.

\textbf{Ask 3 --- why SPAM?} Unmitigated SPAM falsifies the spectroscopy at
14--34$\sigma$; mitigation restores truth exactly (and the double-echo determines
$S_{12,12}(0)$). The gate \emph{design} survives SPAM bias --- the penalty lives
in prediction credibility, which is precisely what the paper quotes.
